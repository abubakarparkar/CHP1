Biology Definitions

Biology Definitions

Chapter 1: Introduction to Biology

Biology:

The scientific study of life and living organisms, encompassing various fields such as genetics, ecology, and physiology.

Cell:

The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms, often referred to as the building blocks of life.

Homeostasis:

The process by which living organisms maintain a stable internal environment despite changes in external conditions.

Chapter 2: Plant Reproduction

Asexual Reproduction:

A mode of reproduction that does not involve the fusion of gametes, resulting in offspring genetically identical to the parent.

Sexual Reproduction:

A reproductive process that involves the fusion of male and female gametes, leading to genetically diverse offspring.

Pollination:

The transfer of pollen from the male anther to the female stigma of a flower, which is essential for fertilization in flowering plants.

Germination:

The process by which a seed develops into a new plant, beginning when the seed absorbs water and ends when the plant emerges from the soil.

Questions and Answers

1. Insect pollinated flowers usually possess:
a. Sticky pollens with rough surface
b. Large quantities of pollens
c. Dry pollens with smooth surface
d. Light coloured pollens
Answer: a. Sticky pollens with rough surface
Sticky and rough surface helps in adhering to the bodies of insects, facilitating pollination.
2. In ovule, meiosis occurs in:
a. Integument
b. Nucellus
c. Megaspore
d. Megaspore mother cell
Answer: d. Megaspore mother cell
Meiosis occurs in the megaspore mother cell, resulting in the formation of megaspores.
3. The ploidy level is NOT the same in:
a. Integuments and nucellus
b. Root tip and shoot tip
c. Secondary nucleus and endosperm
d. Antipodals and synergids
Answer: c. Secondary nucleus and endosperm
The secondary nucleus is triploid (3n) while the endosperm also has a triploid ploidy level but can differ in genetic composition.
4. Which of the following types require a pollinator but result is genetically similar to autogamy?
a. Geitonogamy
b. Xenogamy
c. Apogamy
d. Cleistogamy
Answer: a. Geitonogamy
Geitonogamy involves pollen transfer between flowers of the same plant, resulting in genetically similar offspring.
5. If the diploid chromosome number in a flowering plant is 12, then which one of the following will have 6 chromosomes?
a. Endosperm
b. Leaf cells
c. Cotyledons
d. Synergids
Answer: d. Synergids
Synergids, which are part of the embryo sac, are haploid (n), having half the diploid number (6 chromosomes).
6. In angiosperms, endosperm is formed by/due to:
a. Free nuclear divisions of megaspore
b. Polar nuclei
c. Polar nuclei and male gamete
d. Synergids and male gamete
Answer: c. Polar nuclei and male gamete
The endosperm is formed when one male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei, resulting in a triploid structure.
7. Point out the odd one:
a. Nucellus
b. Embryo sac
c. Micropyle
d. Pollen grain
Answer: d. Pollen grain
Pollen grain is a male gametophyte, while the others are part of the ovule structure.
Very Short Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Very Short Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Questions and Answers

1. Name the part of gynoecium that determines the compatible nature of pollen grain.
Answer: Stigma
The stigma is responsible for recognizing and accepting compatible pollen grains for fertilization.
2. How many haploid cells are present in a mature embryo sac?
Answer: Seven haploid cells
The mature embryo sac consists of seven cells, including one egg cell, two synergids, three antipodal cells, and one central cell with two polar nuclei.
3. Even though each pollen grain has 2 male gametes, why are at least 20 pollen grains required to fertilize 20 ovules in a particular carpel?
Answer: To ensure successful fertilization
Not all pollen grains may be viable or successfully germinate, hence multiple grains are needed to ensure that enough pollen reaches the ovules.
4. Define megasporogenesis.
Answer: The formation of megaspores
Megasporogenesis is the process by which a megasporangium (nucellus) undergoes meiosis to produce four megaspores, of which usually one develops into the embryo sac.
5. What is hydrophily?
Answer: Water pollination
Hydrophily is a form of pollination where pollen is transferred by water, common in aquatic plants.
6. Name the layer which supplies nourishment to the developing pollen grains.
Answer: Tapetum
The tapetum is a nutritive layer that provides essential nutrients to the developing pollen grains in the anther.
7. Define parthenocarpy.
Answer: Fruit development without fertilization
Parthenocarpy is the development of fruit without fertilization, resulting in seedless fruits.
8. Are pollination and fertilization necessary in apomixis?
Answer: No
In apomixis, seeds are formed without fertilization, allowing asexual reproduction.
9. Name the parts of pistil which develop into fruits and seeds.
Answer: Ovary and ovules
The ovary develops into the fruit, while the ovules develop into seeds after fertilization.
10. What is the function of the filiform apparatus?
Answer: To guide pollen tubes
The filiform apparatus, located in the synergids, helps guide the pollen tube to the egg cell during fertilization.
Short Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Short Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Questions and Answers

1. How can polyembryony be commercially exploited?
Answer: Polyembryony can be exploited by producing multiple embryos in a single ovule, leading to the development of several genetically identical seedlings from one fertilized ovule. This is beneficial in agriculture as it allows for the rapid propagation of desirable traits in crops.
For example, in citrus fruits, polyembryonic seeds can produce several plants with the same desirable characteristics, improving yields and consistency in fruit quality.
2. Pollination and seed formation are very crucial for fruit formation. Justify the statement.
Answer: Pollination is essential for transferring pollen from the male anthers to the female stigma, initiating fertilization, which leads to seed development. Seeds are vital for fruit formation as they provide the genetic material necessary for the next generation.
Without successful pollination and subsequent fertilization, ovules do not develop into seeds, and thus, the ovary cannot mature into a fruit, affecting the reproductive success of the plant.
3. Incompatibility is a natural barrier in the fusion of gametes. How will you explain this statement?
Answer: Incompatibility refers to the mechanisms that prevent fertilization between genetically similar or identical gametes. This ensures genetic diversity and prevents inbreeding depression.
In plants, self-incompatibility systems can reject pollen from the same plant, allowing only compatible pollen to fertilize the ovules, which promotes cross-pollination and genetic variation.
4. Describe three devices by which cross-pollination is encouraged in Angiosperms by avoiding self-pollination.
Answer: Three devices to encourage cross-pollination include:
  1. Temporal separation: Some plants have different timing for the maturation of male and female gametes, ensuring that pollen from one flower is released when another flower is receptive.
  2. Structural adaptation: Flowers may possess unique structures, such as position of anthers and stigma, that physically prevent self-pollination.
  3. Genetic mechanisms: Self-incompatibility genes can inhibit the growth of pollen from the same plant, promoting fertilization only with genetically different pollen.
These adaptations help maintain genetic diversity, which is crucial for the resilience and adaptability of plant populations.
Long Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Long Answer Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Questions and Answers

1. Describe the process of double fertilization.
Answer: Double fertilization is a unique process that occurs in angiosperms (flowering plants) during fertilization. It involves the fusion of one male gamete with the egg cell and the other male gamete with two polar nuclei. The steps are as follows:
  • Pollen Tube Formation: After pollination, a pollen grain germinates on the stigma, forming a pollen tube that grows down the style towards the ovule.
  • Entry of Pollen Tube: The pollen tube enters the ovule through the micropyle, releasing two male gametes into the embryo sac.
  • First Fertilization: One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form the diploid zygote, which will develop into the embryo.
  • Second Fertilization: The second male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei, forming a triploid cell that develops into the endosperm, providing nourishment to the developing embryo.
Thus, double fertilization results in the formation of both the embryo and the endosperm, which are essential for the development of seeds in angiosperms.
2. Explain the stages involved in the maturation of microspore into male gametophyte.
Answer: The maturation of microspores into male gametophytes involves several stages:
  • Microsporogenesis: Microspores are produced within the anthers of flowering plants through the meiotic division of microspore mother cells (microsporocytes).
  • Microgametogenesis: Each microspore undergoes mitotic division, resulting in two cells: the generative cell and the tube cell. This process forms a structure called the pollen grain.
  • Pollen Grain Structure: The mature pollen grain consists of the tube cell (which will develop into the pollen tube) and the generative cell (which will divide to form two sperm cells).
  • Development into Male Gametophyte: Upon reaching the stigma of a compatible flower, the pollen grain germinates, and the tube cell elongates to form a pollen tube, while the generative cell divides mitotically to produce two male gametes.
The mature male gametophyte, now consisting of the pollen tube and two sperm cells, is ready for fertilization.
3. Explain the development of dicot embryo.
Answer: The development of a dicot embryo occurs after fertilization and involves several key stages:
  • Zygote Formation: After double fertilization, the zygote forms from the fusion of the male gamete with the egg cell.
  • Embryo Development: The zygote undergoes a series of mitotic divisions to form a multicellular structure. This develops into two distinct regions:
    • Radical: The lower part of the embryo, which will develop into the root.
    • Plumule: The upper part that will form the shoot and leaves.
  • Formation of Cotyledons: In dicots, two cotyledons develop from the embryo. These serve as seed leaves and store nutrients for the developing plant.
  • Seed Maturation: As the embryo matures, it is enclosed within the seed coat, and the surrounding ovule tissue develops into the seed. The ovary wall develops into the fruit.
The mature dicot embryo, with its cotyledons and embryonic axis, is crucial for germination and the establishment of a new plant.

Additional Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Additional Questions - Chapter 1 Biology

Q1: What is the significance of double fertilization in angiosperms?

A1: Double fertilization is significant because it results in the formation of both the zygote and the endosperm, providing nourishment to the developing embryo. This ensures successful seed development and growth.

Q2: Explain the role of pollinators in plant reproduction.

A2: Pollinators, such as bees, butterflies, and birds, facilitate the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma. This process enhances genetic diversity and increases the chances of successful fertilization and seed formation.

Q3: How does self-pollination differ from cross-pollination?

A3: Self-pollination occurs when pollen from the same flower fertilizes its ovules, leading to genetically similar offspring. Cross-pollination involves the transfer of pollen between different flowers, promoting genetic variation and adaptability in the population.

Q4: What adaptations do plants have for wind pollination?

A4: Wind-pollinated plants often have lightweight, smooth pollen grains, exposed anthers, and feathery stigmas to catch drifting pollen. These adaptations enhance the efficiency of pollen transfer through wind currents.
