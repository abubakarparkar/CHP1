Summary of Reproduction in Lower and Higher Animals

Chapter 2 delves into the mesmerizing world of cell biology, exploring the fundamental units of life. It introduces two primary cell types: prokaryotic and eukaryotic, highlighting their structural differences and functions. Prokaryotic cells, found in organisms like bacteria, are simpler and lack a defined nucleus. In contrast, eukaryotic cells, present in plants and animals, are more complex with membrane-bound organelles.

The importance of the cell membrane is also emphasized, acting as a selective barrier that regulates the movement of substances.

Additionally, this chapter touches on the processes of cellular respiration and photosynthesis, illustrating how cells convert energy to sustain life. Understanding these processes lays the groundwork for appreciating the complexity and interconnectivity of life on Earth.

Chapter 2: Biology Definitions

Chapter 2: Definitions in Biology

Cell

The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms, serving as the building block of life.

Prokaryotic Cell

A type of cell that lacks a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles, typically found in bacteria.

Eukaryotic Cell

A more complex cell type that contains a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, found in plants, animals, and fungi.

Organelle

Specialized structures within a cell that perform distinct processes necessary for cellular function.

Cell Membrane

The semi-permeable membrane surrounding the cell, controlling the movement of substances in and out of the cell.

Nucleus

The membrane-bound organelle that contains the cell's genetic material (DNA) and controls its activities.

Mitochondria

The powerhouse of the cell, responsible for producing energy (ATP) through cellular respiration.

Ribosomes

Cellular structures responsible for protein synthesis, found either floating freely in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

A network of membranes involved in protein and lipid synthesis; can be rough (with ribosomes) or smooth (without ribosomes).

Golgi Apparatus

An organelle that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for secretion or delivery to other organelles.

Chloroplast

An organelle found in plant cells that conducts photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy.

Cell Wall

A rigid outer layer found in plant cells, fungi, and bacteria that provides support and protection.

Cytoplasm

The jelly-like substance within the cell membrane that contains organelles and is the site of many cellular processes.

Biology Q&A

Biology Questions and Answers

1. What is the difference between a foetus and an embryo?

A foetus is a later stage of development after 8 weeks of gestation, while an embryo is the early stage from fertilization until 8 weeks.

2. Outline the path of sperm up to the urethra.

Sperm travels from the testes to the epididymis, then through the vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate gland, and finally into the urethra.

3. Which glands contribute fluids to the semen?

The seminal vesicles, prostate gland, and bulbourethral glands contribute fluids to the semen.

4. Name the endocrine glands involved in maintaining the sex characteristics of males.

The testes and adrenal glands are involved in maintaining male sex characteristics.

5. Where does fertilization and implantation occur?

Fertilization occurs in the fallopian tubes, and implantation occurs in the uterus.

6. Enlist the external genital organs in females.

The external genital organs in females include the labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, and vaginal opening.

7. Give two differences between blastula and gastrula.

The blastula is a hollow ball of cells formed after cleavage, while the gastrula is the stage where cells begin to differentiate and form the three germ layers.

8. What is the difference between an embryo and a zygote?

A zygote is the initial cell formed by the fusion of sperm and egg, while an embryo is the developing organism from the zygote stage up to 8 weeks.

Biology Short Answer Questions

Biology Short Answer Questions

1. Write a note on budding in Hydra.

Budding in Hydra is a form of asexual reproduction where a new individual develops from a small outgrowth (bud) on the parent organism. The bud eventually detaches and becomes a fully functional Hydra. This method allows for rapid population increase in favorable conditions.

2. Explain the different methods of reproduction occurring in sponges.

Sponges reproduce both sexually and asexually. Asexually, they can regenerate from fragments or through budding. Sexually, they release sperm into the water, which is captured by another sponge, leading to fertilization and the development of free-swimming larvae.

3. Write a note on IVF.

In vitro fertilization (IVF) is a medical procedure where an egg is fertilized by sperm outside the body in a lab. After fertilization, the embryo is implanted in the uterus. IVF is often used to help couples facing infertility and has resulted in many successful pregnancies.

4. Comment on any two mechanical contraceptive methods.

Two mechanical contraceptive methods are:
  • Condoms: Worn on the penis or inside the vagina, they create a barrier that prevents sperm from entering the uterus, reducing the risk of pregnancy and STIs.
  • Diaphragms: A dome-shaped cup inserted into the vagina to cover the cervix, preventing sperm from reaching the egg. It should be used with spermicide for higher effectiveness.

5. Write a note on tubectomy.

Tubectomy is a surgical procedure for female sterilization, where the fallopian tubes are cut, tied, or blocked. This prevents eggs from traveling from the ovaries to the uterus, effectively preventing pregnancy. Tubectomy is considered a permanent form of contraception.

6. Give the name of the causal organism of syphilis and write on its symptoms.

Syphilis is caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. Symptoms vary by stage: the primary stage typically presents as a painless sore (chancre), the secondary stage includes rashes and flu-like symptoms, while the tertiary stage can affect multiple organ systems, leading to severe complications if untreated.

7. What is colostrum?

Colostrum is the first milk produced by mammals after giving birth. It is rich in antibodies and nutrients, providing essential immune protection and nourishment to newborns during the first few days of life before the mature milk comes in.
Biology Questions and Answers

Biology Questions and Answers

1. Phases of Menstrual Cycle and Their Hormonal Control

The menstrual cycle consists of four main phases:

  • Menstrual Phase (Day 1-5): The endometrial lining sheds, leading to menstruation. Estrogen and progesterone levels are low.
  • Follicular Phase (Day 6-14): Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH) stimulates follicle growth. Estrogen levels rise, stimulating endometrial repair.
  • Ovulation (Day 14): A surge in Luteinizing Hormone (LH) causes the release of a mature egg from the ovary.
  • Luteal Phase (Day 15-28): Corpus luteum forms and secretes progesterone, which thickens the endometrium for potential implantation.

2. Steps of Parturition

Parturition, or childbirth, involves three stages:

  • Dilation Stage: The cervix dilates and thins, facilitated by uterine contractions.
  • Expulsion Stage: The baby is pushed out of the uterus through the birth canal.
  • Placental Stage: The placenta is expelled from the uterus after the baby is born.

3. Histological Structure of Testis

The testis has several distinct features:

  • Seminiferous Tubules: These coiled tubes are the site of sperm production.
  • Interstitial (Leydig) Cells: Located between the tubules, they secrete testosterone.
  • Sertoli Cells: Support and nourish developing sperm cells.

4. Structure of Blastula

The blastula is a hollow ball of cells formed after fertilization. It consists of:

  • Blastocoel: The central fluid-filled cavity.
  • Blastomeres: The individual cells forming the outer layer.
  • Trophoblast: A layer that eventually forms the placenta.

5. Histological Structure of Ovary

The ovary contains:

  • Ovarian Follicles: Structures containing the developing oocytes (eggs).
  • Corpus Luteum: A structure formed after ovulation that secretes progesterone.
  • Stroma: Connective tissue that provides structural support.

6. Methods of Birth Control to Avoid Pregnancy

There are several methods of birth control:

  • Barrier Methods: Condoms and diaphragms prevent sperm from reaching the egg.
  • Hormonal Methods: Birth control pills, patches, and injections prevent ovulation.
  • IUD (Intrauterine Device): A device placed in the uterus to prevent fertilization or implantation.
  • Sterilization: Tubal ligation (for women) and vasectomy (for men) are permanent methods.
  • Natural Methods: Tracking fertility and abstaining during ovulation.

7. Goals of RCH (Reproductive and Child Health) Programme

The goals of the RCH programme are:

  • To improve reproductive health.
  • To reduce maternal and infant mortality.
  • To promote family planning and birth control.
  • To prevent and manage sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

8. Hormones Involved in Parturition

Several hormones regulate parturition:

  • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions.
  • Relaxin: Relaxes the pelvic ligaments and softens the cervix.
  • Prostaglandins: Help induce labor by increasing contractions.

9. Functions of Male Accessory Glands

The male accessory glands, including the prostate gland, seminal vesicles, and bulbourethral glands, serve to:

  • Prostate Gland: Secretes a fluid that nourishes sperm and protects them from the acidic environment of the female reproductive tract.
  • Seminal Vesicles: Produce a fructose-rich fluid that provides energy for sperm.
  • Bulbourethral Glands: Secrete a lubricating fluid that neutralizes acidity in the urethra.

10. What is Capacitation? Its Importance

Capacitation is the process by which sperm undergoes physiological changes to become capable of fertilizing an egg. It involves:

  • Changes in the sperm’s plasma membrane, allowing it to bind to the egg.
  • Increased motility, enhancing its ability to reach and penetrate the egg.

Capacitation is crucial for successful fertilization.

Male Reproductive System

Male Reproductive System

Explanation of Parts

The male reproductive system consists of several structures that work together to produce and transport sperm. Here is a detailed explanation of key parts:

  • Testis: The testes are oval-shaped organs located in the scrotum. They produce sperm and secrete testosterone, the hormone responsible for male characteristics and sperm production.
  • Vasa Deferentia: These are the ducts that transport sperm from the epididymis to the urethra. They play a crucial role in ejaculation.
  • Epididymis: A long, coiled tube located on the back of each testis. Sperm mature and are stored here before being transported through the vas deferens.
  • Seminal Vesicle: These glands produce a fluid that makes up a significant portion of semen. The fluid contains fructose, which provides energy for sperm.
  • Prostate Gland: This gland surrounds the urethra below the bladder. It secretes a fluid that nourishes and protects sperm during ejaculation.
  • Penis: The external organ used for sexual intercourse and the passage of urine. It contains the urethra, which carries sperm during ejaculation.

Female Reproductive System

Female Reproductive System

Explanation of Parts

The female reproductive system is designed to produce eggs, facilitate fertilization, and support the development of a fetus. Here are the main components:

  • Ovaries: These are small, almond-shaped organs located on each side of the uterus. The ovaries produce eggs (ova) and secrete hormones like estrogen and progesterone.
  • Fallopian Tubes: These are narrow tubes that transport the egg from the ovary to the uterus. Fertilization typically occurs in the fallopian tubes.
  • Uterus: A hollow, muscular organ where the fertilized egg implants and develops into a fetus. The lining of the uterus (endometrium) thickens each month in preparation for pregnancy.
  • Cervix: The lower, narrow part of the uterus that opens into the vagina. It allows the flow of menstrual blood and dilates during childbirth.
  • Vagina: The muscular canal that connects the cervix to the external genitalia. It serves as the passage for childbirth and menstruation, and the site of sperm deposition during intercourse.

Fertilization and Zygote Division

Fertilization and Zygote Division

1. Process of Fertilization

Fertilization is the process by which a sperm cell fuses with an egg cell (ovum) to form a zygote. The steps of fertilization are as follows:

  • Sperm Migration: The sperm travels through the female reproductive tract, reaching the fallopian tube where the egg is present.
  • Sperm-Egg Fusion: The head of the sperm binds to the egg and releases enzymes that allow it to penetrate the outer layers of the egg, leading to fusion of their nuclei.
  • Formation of Zygote: Once the sperm enters the egg, the genetic material from both cells combines, forming a single-cell zygote with a full set of chromosomes.

2. Zygote Division into Morula

After fertilization, the zygote undergoes a series of cell divisions (cleavage) to form a solid ball of cells known as the morula. This process occurs as follows:

  • First Cleavage: The zygote divides into two cells (blastomeres) approximately 24 hours after fertilization.
  • Subsequent Cleavages: The two blastomeres divide into four, then eight, and so on, through repeated mitotic divisions.
  • Morula Formation: After about 16-32 cells are formed, the embryo reaches the morula stage, which is a compact ball of cells.
